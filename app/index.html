Chapter 01 · where people are

340 million people, very unevenly spread.

Population per square kilometre runs from empty western counties to Manhattan across four orders of magnitude. A handful of dense counties hold a huge share of the country — which is exactly why the color breaks you choose matter so much.

Chapter 02 · the money map

Income is a geography, not a list.

The coasts and the big-metro suburbs separate cleanly from the rural interior and the Deep South. Switch to a quantile cut and the structure sharpens — the first hint of the spatial dependence we measure later.

Chapter 03 · the persistent South

Poverty clusters, county by county.

Drop into the South and a band of high-poverty counties resolves: the Mississippi Delta, the Black Belt, Appalachia, the Texas border. Poverty is not scattered at random; it pools. That is the difference between a map and a dataset of independent dots.

Chapter 04 · who lives where

Demography and disadvantage share a map.

Lay the Black-share map beside the poverty map and the overlap across the Black Belt is hard to miss. Correlation, not destiny — but the co-location is the empirical fact every model in this atlas has to reckon with.

Chapter 05 · the health gradient

A health gradient you can see.

Adult diabetes prevalence rises across the same southern counties — the "diabetes belt" the CDC named. This is where the redlining story begins: places that were starved of investment decades ago still carry the health cost today. Take the controls and test it yourself.
